## Conversation

### K-1 Taxation

**User:** Is there anything about the K-1 taxation for 10Fed?

**Assistant:** Yes. For 10FSSAC5:

- Investors hold an LLC taxed as a partnership, so they receive Schedule K-1, despite an underlying subsidiary electing REIT status.
- The pitchbook promises one K-1 by March 15 and no state K-1s.
- Legal documents are less favorable: K-1 delivery could occur after April 15, requiring extensions.
- The PPM says investors may still owe state taxes, face withholding, and need state returns, particularly where assets are held outside the REIT.
- Taxable income can be allocated without matching cash distributions. Tax distributions are discretionary, not guaranteed.
- Tax-exempt investors should expect some possible UBTI.
- Foreign investors face additional withholding, ECI/FIRPTA, filing, and possible estate-tax issues.

The "single K-1/no state filings" claim should be confirmed in writing because the PPM materially qualifies it.

### Accelerated Depreciation

**User:** Is there anything about fast depreciation?

**Assistant:** Not explicitly. The materials contain no promise of cost segregation, accelerated depreciation, or bonus-depreciation benefits for investors.

- Most assets are expected to sit inside the subsidiary REIT.
- REIT depreciation losses do not pass through to investors on their K-1.
- Losses generated outside the REIT may pass through, but generally constitute passive losses subject to deduction limitations.
- Depreciation may later create recapture income when assets are sold.
- The PPM says management had not decided whether to make a tax election that could require slower depreciation and prohibit bonus depreciation.

This does not appear structured as a typical syndication offering large upfront depreciation deductions to limited partners. Any projected first-year K-1 loss or cost-segregation benefit should be requested explicitly from 10 Federal.

### Distribution Frequency

**User:** What is the distribution frequency?

**Assistant:** The stated distribution frequency is quarterly.

- Distributions are subject to available cash and the sponsor's discretion.
- The company targets a 5% annual current distribution, paid quarterly.
- Distributions may initially be supplemented using investor capital.
- Unpaid portions of the 8% preferred return accrue for later payment.

Quarterly is the intended cadence, not a guaranteed quarterly payment.

### Preferred Return

**User:** When is the preferred return achieved?

**Assistant:** The 8% preferred return accrues annually on unreturned invested capital, compounded annually.

- Quarterly operating distributions first cover accrued preferred return.
- Any unpaid amount continues accruing.
- A sale or refinancing distribution also pays accrued preferred return before returning capital and paying the sponsor promote.

The targeted 5% annual quarterly distribution is only a partial current payment; the remaining preferred return generally accrues. The 8% return is not guaranteed.

### Target 15% Investor IRR

**User:** How can we go to 15%?

**Assistant:** The 15% is a target investor IRR, not a 15% annual cash distribution.

It would be reached through:

- Quarterly operating distributions, targeted around 5% annually.
- Accrued 8% preferred return.
- Return of invested capital.
- Appreciation proceeds from refinancing or selling properties.

After the 8% preferred return and capital treatment specified by the waterfall, additional profits are generally split 70% to investors and 30% to the sponsor until investors reach the 15% compounded hurdle. Remaining profits are then split 60% to investors and 40% to the sponsor.

Reaching 15% depends mainly on successful property sales or refinancing. Quarterly distributions alone would not achieve it, and the 15% target is not guaranteed.

### REIT Status And The K-1

**User:** What is the REIT status in relation to the K-1?

**Assistant:** The structure has two tax layers:

1. The investor holds an interest in 10FSSAC5-A, LLC, which is taxed as a partnership and issues Schedule K-1.
2. The LLC invests mainly through 10FSSAC5 REIT, a subsidiary electing REIT tax status.

The REIT's income, such as dividends or capital-gain distributions, flows into the LLC. The LLC then reports the investor's allocated share on the K-1.

The key consequence is that the REIT acts as a tax blocker: property-level depreciation and losses generally remain inside the REIT and do not pass through directly to the investor's K-1. This explains why the offering can simultaneously have REIT status and issue investors K-1s.

### REIT Tax Advantages

**User:** What is a REIT, and is there any tax advantage for the REIT?

**Assistant:** A REIT, or real estate investment trust, is an entity that owns real estate and meets specific income, asset, ownership, and distribution requirements.

For 10FSSAC5, the REIT is a subsidiary beneath the partnership that issues the investor's K-1.

Potential tax advantages include:

- The REIT generally avoids federal corporate income tax on income it distributes, preventing much of the normal corporate double taxation.
- Ordinary REIT dividends may qualify eligible non-corporate taxpayers for a Section 199A deduction, subject to current tax law and investor circumstances.
- The structure may reduce multistate filing obligations for investors.
- It may block much of the UBTI that tax-exempt or retirement-account investors could otherwise receive.
- Depreciation can reduce the REIT's taxable income and may cause part of its distributions to be treated as return of capital.

The trade-off is that property-level depreciation and losses generally stay inside the REIT and do not pass directly through to investors' K-1s.

### Investment Through An LLC Taxed As A C Corporation

**User:** If I have an LLC taxed as a C corporation, what are the tax implications for investing in a REIT?

**Assistant:** For an LLC taxed as a C corporation investing in 10FSSAC5:

- The LLC receives a K-1 because it owns the partnership above the REIT.
- Allocated taxable income is reported on Form 1120 and generally taxed at the applicable federal corporate rate, even if cash distributions are lower.
- REIT dividends generally do not qualify for the corporate dividends-received deduction.
- A C corporation cannot use the individual Section 199A deduction for REIT dividends.
- Property depreciation and losses inside the REIT generally do not pass through to the corporation.
- Losses generated outside the REIT may pass through, but basis and potentially passive-activity limitations apply.
- The REIT structure may reduce state filing exposure, but the PPM does not guarantee no state filings or withholding.
- If the C corporation later distributes after-tax proceeds to its shareholders, that can create a second tax layer at shareholder level.

For a C-corporation investor, the principal advantages may be state-filing simplification and containing property operations inside the REIT rather than accelerated depreciation or a preferential federal rate.
