Reliability note: This is a secondary interpretation, not a sponsor, tax-counsel, or IRS opinion. The structural and economic claims are broadly consistent with the existing pitchbook, PPM, and LLC-agreement summaries, but the tax conclusions have not been independently verified against the full legal documents or the investor's facts.

## Key Takeaways

- Investors own interests in 10FSSAC5-A, LLC, a partnership that issues Schedule K-1, while substantially all assets are expected to be held through a subsidiary electing REIT status.
- The REIT structure explains why investors can receive a K-1 while the underlying property vehicle is a REIT.
- Property-level depreciation and losses inside the REIT generally do not pass through directly to investors. The materials do not promise cost segregation, accelerated depreciation, or bonus-depreciation benefits.
- The intended distribution cadence is quarterly, but distributions are discretionary and may be supplemented with investor capital.
- The 8% preferred return accrues on unreturned Class I capital and is compounded annually. A stated 5% annual current distribution would leave part of the preferred return accrued.
- The 15% figure is a target investor IRR dependent on operating cash flow, refinancing, and sale proceeds, not a promised annual cash yield.
- The pitchbook's single-K-1, March 15, and no-state-K-1 statement is qualified by the legal documents. The PPM warns that K-1s may arrive after April 15 and that investors may still face state tax, withholding, or filing obligations.
- The conversation states that an LLC taxed as a C corporation would report allocated K-1 items on Form 1120, generally could not use the individual Section 199A deduction, and generally would not receive a corporate dividends-received deduction for REIT dividends. These investor-specific conclusions require tax-adviser confirmation.
- A C-corporation investment may create taxation at the corporate level and another tax layer when proceeds are distributed to shareholders.
