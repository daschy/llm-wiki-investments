## Key Extracted Points

- Private-company secondary platforms commonly restrict purchases to accredited investors and impose KYC, AML, tax, beneficial-ownership, source-of-funds, suitability, and deal-level checks.
- SEC guidance recognizes entities whose equity owners are all accredited investors as an accredited-investor category.
- The source suggests that the LLC might qualify through its ownership chain, but it does not resolve whether the two Dutch BVs must each independently qualify or whether a platform will look through them to the ultimate natural-person owners.
- Platform access does not ensure transaction eligibility. Issuer consent, rights of first refusal, transfer restrictions, buyer approval, minimum investment amounts, jurisdiction support, and the choice between direct shares and an SPV or fund can still control.
- A likely onboarding package includes LLC and BV formation documents, shareholder registers, an ownership chart, beneficial-owner identification, investment authority, accreditation evidence, source-of-funds support, and applicable tax forms.
- Maintaining the LLC as a genuine general investment vehicle may support onboarding credibility. It does not substitute for satisfying the applicable accredited-investor category or platform rules.

## Critical Open Items

- Obtain securities-counsel advice on applying Rule 501(a) to a U.S. LLC whose direct equity owners are foreign corporations.
- Ask each platform whether it accepts the LLC/BV ownership chain, which entity or individuals must establish accreditation, and which jurisdictions it supports.
- Confirm whether the platform will issue a W-9 or require a W-8 series form based on the LLC's actual U.S. federal tax classification; do not infer the form from formation jurisdiction alone.
- Confirm deal-specific issuer approval, transfer restrictions, minimums, fees, custody, reporting, and liquidity mechanics.
- Verify current platform terms and onboarding requirements before relying on the conversation.
